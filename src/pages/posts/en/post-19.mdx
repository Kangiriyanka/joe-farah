## Focus 
<br/>
After pondering over the concepts inside productivity books on focus, habits, etc., observing what goes on through your mind in a state of focus is the facet of productivity I value the most

 Productivity is a broad subject, 
so allow me to first define it in terms of a question:

<div class="m-auto text-center my-2 p-3">

<p class="bold-rounded">"In a session <span class="text-green-500">X</span> focusing on task  <span class="text-green-500">Y</span>, how efficient will I be at producing <span class="text-green-500">Z</span>?". </p>
</div>


Finding answers to the above is limitless. For starters, the session is <span class="text-green-500">X</span> variable, i.e., your environment and 
the time given to carry out the task. Likewise, the type of task influences your focus <span class="text-green-500">Y</span>. 
For example, if you're learning something unfamiliar or beyond your skill level, you might feel uneasy and distracted as
 opposed to something you've been doing for awhile. Lastly, there's the result you're expecting to produce: what do you want out ? My point is that there are many ways to optimize for efficiency hence the multitude of books that exist of that genre. Everyone, as do I, has their say on it, and packages it the way they see fit for you to digest. 


<br/>
Personally, when I sit down and work for a time period, I value above all the creation of an internal space where I can <span class="text-green-500"> focus </span> without judging myself on efficiency or ability. If I'm practicing a difficult song on the guitar for 30 minutes, I'll make an effort to not react negatively if I can't play a certain chord. Those non-judgemental focused sessions are the facets I care about when I think of productivity, 
because they're smallest units I can control. It makes a difference for who I am. Specifically, since I'm the kind of person that tends to be critical and hard on themselves, my focus dissipates when I react hastily to my inability to nail things down in timely fashion. Thus, being kind and loose to myself works wonders and is a continuous process.




&nbsp;

## What about you?


 It's a good idea to question your stance in all this, that is, asking yourself what truly makes you productive. After you’ve read relevant books, after you’ve devised plans and established good habits, it’s your responsibility to assess what works, and to be honest about it. With time, you’ll continuously build and adapt your methodology and tell yourself: “This works, because I made it work my way through trial and error”. Granted, being consistent in your methods is a beast in its own right, because life happens which changes your priorities and influences your mood.
